How to choose the right spirit level

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Before you buy a spirit level in 2026, understand that accuracy, durability, and length should match your specific projects, don’t overspend on a digital level if a quality analog level will do, and always look for a stated accuracy of ±0.5 mm/m or better for general construction work.

What makes a spirit level accurate and reliable?

Accuracy usually isn’t measured in degrees but in millimetres per metre (mm/m). A quality level will be rated to within 0.5 mm/m or 0.029°. anything looser than 1.0 mm/m will let you down on long runs. Reliable levels are milled from a single piece of aluminium or a precision-formed box section, so the reference edge stays true. Check that the vial is securely bedded in a shock-absorbing mount, and that the bubble window hasn’t been glued in by the factory – properly manufactured units are often machined with a tight press-fit. The best way to verify accuracy without a lab is to place the level on a flat, level surface, note the bubble position, then flip the level end-for-end. If the bubble sits in exactly the same spot, you’ve got a keeper. Reputable brands like Stabila, Stanley, and Empire have long track records here, but you can find excellent budget-friendly options from smaller makers that meet the same standard.

Which spirit level type should you buy for different jobs?

The three mainstream designs are box-beam, I-beam, and torpedo levels. Box-beam levels are the sturdiest, they resist twisting under heavy use and are ideal for framing, decking, and concrete work. Their rectangular cross-section gives them a lot of rigidity. I-beam levels are lighter, easier to hold overhead, and perfectly adequate for carpentry and trim work, though they can flex if you drop them on a corner. Torpedo levels are short, compact tools meant for tight spots like inside cabinets or between studs. Many now include a strong magnet along the base, which is brilliant for levelling metal studs, pipes, or electrical boxes. For general home maintenance, a 600 mm (24‑inch) box-beam level with a magnetic edge will cover most bases. If you do a lot of plumbing or heavy framing, add a 1200 mm (48‑inch) level later.

What length of spirit level do you actually need?

Length matters because a longer level spans more surface and averages out small bumps, a 1800 mm (6‑foot) level will show you a dip that a 600 mm level might miss. For everyday home use, a 600 mm size is a good starter: it fits in a toolbox and works on shelves, pictures, and small furniture. A 1200 mm level is the go-to for hanging cabinets, aligning doors, and checking countertops. Professional tilers and masons often buy levels that are 1500 mm or longer because they need to bridge across uneven substrates. But a level that is too long becomes awkward to store and carry. A practical three-level kit might include a 400 mm torpedo for tight spots, a 600 mm general‑purpose, and a 1200 mm for longer runs. You can always use a shorter level and a straightedge, but it’s less convenient.

Are digital spirit levels worth the extra money?

Digital levels add an electronic inclinometer that gives you a numeric readout, often to 0.1°. They are genuinely useful when you need to transfer an angle from one surface to another, say, for stair stringers, ramp slopes, or aligning solar panels. They also let you set a target angle (like 5° for drainage) and get instant feedback, which is far more precise than eyeballing a bubble. However, digital levels are more expensive, they rely on batteries that can die mid‑job, and the electronics are less rugged than a simple vial. Many pros keep an analog level for rough work and pull out the digital only for critical angle‑setting. If the majority of your projects are just “is this flat?,” skip the digital and spend the savings on a better analog level with finely ground vials.

How do vials and magnification affect readability?

The vial, the glass tube, is the heart of any spirit level. Better vials have a slightly curved interior radius that makes the bubble respond quickly and settle precisely. Look for vials that are securely bonded to the frame, not loose or rattling. Some levels offer an acrylic window that magnifies the bubble, which can reduce eye strain, especially in dimly lit basements or garages. A common trap is buying a level with tiny, unreadable vials. If you’re middle-aged or wear glasses, choose one with extra‑large vials (often called “big‑eye” or “bull’s‑eye”). These are far easier to read at a glance and make a real difference when you are reaching overhead or working in awkward positions. The trade‑off is slightly larger overall thickness, but the convenience usually wins.

When do you need a magnetic spirit level?

Magnetic levels have a row of rare‑earth magnets embedded in the base or a removable metal strip. They are invaluable for metal stud framing, installing steel beams, or working on metal doors and windows. The magnets hold the level steady so it will not slip while you mark your line. They also free up both hands for marking or fastening. The magnet strength varies widely. Very cheap magnetic levels may have weak magnets that barely hold the level in place, look for ones that can support the tool’s own weight vertically on a steel beam without sliding. Some high‑end models let you switch the magnetism off with a sliding strip, which prevents metal filings from sticking to the base and scratching fine surfaces. For drywall and wood work, non‑magnetic levels are lighter and cheaper.

Frequently asked questions

What does ±0.5 mm/m really mean for my work?

It means the reading could be off by half a millimetre over one metre of length. That is sufficient for almost all construction and finishing work. For very precise machinery levelling, you might need ±0.2 mm/m, but that is overkill for hanging pictures or setting cabinets.

Can I rely on the built-in magnet of a spirit level for pipe work?

Only if the level is specifically designed for pipes, some have a V‑groove cut into the base to centre the level on a round surface, and the magnet holds it in place. A flat‑base magnetic level will sit awkwardly on a pipe and may give a false reading.

How do I protect a spirit level from getting damaged on a job site?

Store it in a padded sheath or dedicated toolbox slot. Do not drop it on its ends, that knocks the vials out of alignment. Wipe off wet concrete or plaster immediately; dried material can scratch the reference edge and ruin accuracy.

Are expensive European brands genuinely better than budget ones?

Brands like Stabila, Sola, and Kapro invest in tighter manufacturing tolerances, better vial fluid that resists cold, and stronger extrusions. A budget level may be fine for occasional home use, but for daily professional use the durability and consistent accuracy justify the higher price.

What is the difference between a machined and a milled level?

Machined means the level’s reference edges are cut to precise flatness and squareness after extrusion. Milled is similar but often refers to the face being cut away to reduce weight. Both are common; the key is that the working edges are true, check the flatness on a known straight surface before buying.

Can I use a 2‑foot level to check if a long countertop is level?

Yes, but you need to move the level along the counter and compare readings at multiple spots. A longer level would show an overall slope more readily. For countertops, a 4‑foot level is ideal, but a 2‑foot level with careful overlapping checks can work.

In shortChoose your spirit level by matching its type, length, and accuracy to the jobs you actually do, not the ones you might imagine. A single well‑made 600 mm box‑beam level will handle most home fixes, while a dedicated magnetic torpedo and a longer 1200 mm version will cover the rest. Spend your money on a level that holds its bubble true and feels solid in your hand, and it will serve you for years.
