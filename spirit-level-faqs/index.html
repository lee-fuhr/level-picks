Spirit Level questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Spirit levels come in various lengths, materials, and accuracy grades, so the best choice depends on the task at hand. For general household projects, a medium-length level with solid construction and clearly marked vials is a safe bet. Always check that the level is true before buying by comparing it against a known flat surface or a trusted reference.

What types of spirit levels are there?

Most spirit levels fall into one of three categories: box beam, I-beam, and torpedo. Box beam levels have a rectangular cross-section and are very durable, making them a favorite for heavy-duty job sites. I-beam levels are lighter and often preferred for finish work because they’re easier to handle. Torpedo levels are compact and fit in tight spaces like inside cabinets or behind pipes. Some specialty levels include digital ones with a readout, magnetic levels for metal studs, and levels with replaceable vials. For most DIYers, a good box beam or I-beam level in a versatile length covers the basics well.

How accurate should a spirit level be?

Accuracy is measured in thousandths of an inch of deviation per inch of length. Entry-level levels for casual home use typically have a moderate accuracy rating, while professional-grade models are much tighter. For hanging pictures or simple shelving, a standard accuracy is fine. For framing, cabinetry, or tile work, you want a level tested to a higher standard. Many reputable brands publish accuracy specs on their packaging. A level that can be easily checked against a known flat surface is always a good idea, and some allow for recalibration if a vial gets knocked out of alignment.

What materials are best for spirit levels?

The frame material affects weight, durability, and accuracy. Aluminum is common, it’s lightweight and resists rust, but it can dent. High-quality levels use aircraft-grade aluminum or even magnesium alloys for a better strength-to-weight ratio. Wooden levels are still made by a few specialty brands; they are traditional but can warp over time in humid conditions. Some budget levels use plastic, which is fine for occasional use but can flex and lose accuracy quickly. For most work, a well-made aluminum level offers the best balance of durability and portability.

What length level do I need?

Length is one of the most important choices. A very short level (a torpedo size) is handy for tight spots, but won’t span across uneven surfaces. A medium length is the most versatile for general home tasks like leveling shelves, appliances, and small fixtures. Long levels are needed for checking longer spans, such as countertops, doors, and floor framing. Many pros carry two or three lengths: a short level for inside cabinets, a medium one for everyday use, and a longer level for structural work. If you can only afford one, a medium length is the most useful starting point.

How do I read a spirit level correctly?

The key is the bubble: when the bubble sits exactly between the two reference lines on the vial, the surface is level. For vertical plumb, the bubble should center in the same way. Many levels have a plumb vial set at 90 degrees to the main vial. Always view the bubble at eye level to avoid parallax error. Some vials have a slight taper, the bubble will move more slowly near the center, which improves readability. When checking a surface, place the level down, read the bubble once, then rotate the level 180 degrees and check again. If the reading is the same, the level is true.

How should I care for and store a spirit level?

Protect the vials from impacts and extreme temperature changes, which can cause the fluid to expand or contract and affect accuracy. Store your level in a dry place, ideally in a padded case or hung on a wall where it won’t get knocked. Clean the frame with a damp cloth and mild soap; avoid harsh solvents that can damage the vial seals. Check your level for trueness periodically by placing it on a known flat surface or doing the 180-degree test. If you drop it heavily, test it before trusting it on a precision job.

Frequently asked questions

Are digital levels better than traditional ones?

Digital levels offer a numeric readout and often have features like audible indicators, memory, and angle measurement. They are convenient for precise work like setting a miter saw or checking slopes. However, they rely on batteries and can be knocked out of calibration if dropped. A good traditional level is more rugged and never runs out of power.

Can I calibrate a spirit level?

Some high-end levels have adjustable vials that allow you to recalibrate them. Most standard levels are not user-serviceable. If a level is consistently off when you do the 180-degree test, the vial is likely damaged or out of spec, and it is usually more cost-effective to replace the level than to attempt a repair.

What is a box beam level?

A box beam level has a rectangular, enclosed cross-section, making it very stiff and resistant to twisting. This design is common on professional job sites because it holds its accuracy well even under rough handling. The extra material does add weight, but many users find the trade-off worthwhile for durability.

What does a plumb bob level do?

A plumb bob level is not a separate tool because most spirit levels already have a plumb vial for checking vertical surfaces. The term sometimes refers to a level that includes a built-in plumb bob (a weight on a string) for reference, but this is rare in modern levels. In practice, you use the plumb vial on any good spirit level to check if a surface is truly vertical.

How often should I replace a level?

Replace a level when it no longer reads true on the 180-degree test or if the vials become cloudy, cracked, or leak fluid. If you use a level daily on construction sites, it might need replacement every year or two. For occasional home use, a quality level can last many years if stored carefully. Toss it if the frame is bent or twisted.

Why do some levels have magnets?

Magnetic levels have rare-earth magnets embedded in the frame so they stick to metal surfaces like steel studs, conduit, or HVAC ducts. This frees both hands for marking or adjusting. The magnets are a huge convenience for electrical and metal-framing work, but they add weight and can attract metal debris. If you rarely work on metal, a standard level is lighter and cheaper.

In shortChoosing the right spirit level comes down to understanding your typical projects and knowing which features matter most: accuracy, length, durability, and special add-ons like magnets. A single mid-size level with a solid reputation can handle the vast majority of household and workshop tasks. Once you own a good level, check it occasionally for trueness, keep it clean, and it will serve you well for years.
